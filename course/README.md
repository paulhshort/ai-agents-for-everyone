# AI Agents & OpenAI Codex: A 2.5-Day Training Course

## Course Title
**"From Chat to Agent: Mastering AI with OpenAI Codex"**

## Course Overview

This hands-on course takes students from zero AI knowledge to confidently using AI agents
to accomplish real tasks. Over 2.5 days, students move from understanding what AI is, to
installing and running their own AI agent, to building real projects and thinking critically
about the future of AI tools.

This is not a lecture course. Students will have Codex running on their machines by the
afternoon of Day 1, and by Day 3 they will present something they built with it.

## Target Audience

- High school students (grades 9-12) and early college students
- No programming experience required
- At most basic ChatGPT experience (casual use counts)
- Comfortable with: texting, social media, YouTube, gaming
- Not yet familiar with: tokens, context windows, agents, CLI tools

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this course, students will be able to:

1. **Explain** how large language models work at a conceptual level (tokens, prediction, context)
2. **Distinguish** between a chatbot and an AI agent
3. **Install and authenticate** OpenAI Codex on their own machine
4. **Write effective prompts** using the WHAT-WHERE-HOW-VERIFY formula
5. **Navigate sandbox and permission modes** and explain why they matter
6. **Manage context** using /status, /compact, and fresh-context patterns
7. **Build a real project** using Codex as their AI agent
8. **Think critically** about AI safety, academic honesty, and the future of AI tools

## Prerequisites

### For Students
- A laptop (Windows, macOS, or Linux)
- Node.js 22 or later installed (provide install guide link in advance)
- A free OpenAI account (or the ability to create one)
- Curiosity and willingness to experiment

### For the Instructor
- All of the above, plus a working Codex installation tested before class
- Projector or large display for live demos
- Whiteboard and markers
- Printed handouts (see `course/handouts/`)
- Pre-prepared mystery folder zip for Lab 1 (see `course/labs/lab-01-explore.md`)
- Reliable internet connection (critical -- have a mobile hotspot as backup)

## Instructor Notes

### Pacing
- Every timestamp is a guideline, not a hard rule. Read the room.
- If students are deeply engaged in a demo, extend it. If eyes are glazing, move on.
- Each day plan marks optional cut points ("if running behind, cut X").

### Energy Management
- Day 1 afternoon is when energy dips hardest. That is why Lab 1 is hands-on.
- Day 2 morning rides the momentum of "I installed a thing and it worked."
- Day 3 is intentionally a half day -- final projects lose quality after 3 hours.

### Common Pitfalls
- School firewalls may block npm. Have the Codex installer on a USB drive.
- Students will try to break things. This is good. Channel it into the safety module.
- Some students will race ahead. Have "stretch goals" ready in each lab.
- Authentication can be flaky on school networks. Budget 15 extra minutes for Day 1 install.

### Tone
- Conversational, not professorial. "Let me show you something cool" not "In this module."
- Celebrate every small win. The first time a student's Codex creates a file is magical.
- Use "we" language. "Let's try this" not "You should do this."
- Mistakes during live demos are teaching moments, not failures.

### Accessibility
- Keep terminal font size large (18pt minimum) for projector visibility.
- Narrate everything you type -- not everyone can read the screen at the same speed.
- Provide printed handouts for students who learn better on paper.
- Allow students to work in pairs if they prefer.
