# Day 3 (Half Day): "Your AI Future" -- Final Projects and What's Next

**Duration:** 9:00 AM - 12:00 PM (3 hours)
**Goal:** Students build a real project with Codex, present it to the class, and leave with a clear picture of where AI is heading and how to keep learning.

---

## 9:00 - 9:15 | Final Project Briefing (15 min)

### Energy Check (3 min)
**Say:** "Last day. This is the day you build something you're proud of and show it off. Who came in with a project idea?"

Take a quick poll. Expect about half the class to have an idea, half undecided.

### Project Options (7 min)
**Display:** `course/labs/lab-03-final-project.md`

For students who need ideas, present these options:

**1. Personal Portfolio Website**
Build a multi-page personal website showcasing who you are. Include an about page, interests, and a "what I learned in this course" section. Great for anyone with even a slight interest in having their own site.

**2. Study Guide Generator**
Pick an upcoming test or a subject you're studying. Use Codex to create a comprehensive study guide with key concepts, practice questions, and summaries. Practical and immediately useful.

**3. Data Explorer**
Find a dataset you care about -- sports stats, music chart data, movie ratings, weather data -- and use Codex to analyze it and create a visual report. Good for the analytically curious.

**4. Task Automator**
Think of something tedious you do on your computer regularly. Rename files? Organize folders? Format notes? Build a tool with Codex that does it for you. Best for students who want to feel the power of automation.

**5. Creative Writing Project**
Use Codex to build an interactive story (HTML-based choose-your-own-adventure), a poetry generator, or a creative writing tool. Perfect for the writers and storytellers.

**6. Your Own Idea**
Pitch it. If it's feasible in 90 minutes with Codex, go for it.

**Say:** "If you're stuck, go with Option 1 -- everyone can build a portfolio page. If you're ambitious, Options 3 or 6 will stretch you."

### Form Teams (5 min)
**Say:** "You can work alone or in pairs. If you pair up, you should both be at the keyboard at different points. No passengers."

Give them 2 minutes to find a partner or decide to go solo. If someone is left out, pair them yourself or let them work solo with extra instructor support.

## 9:15 - 11:00 | Lab 3: Final Project (1 hr 45 min)

### Getting Started (first 15 min is critical)

**Say:** "Before you start typing prompts, take 3 minutes to plan. Write down on paper -- not in Codex yet -- three things:"

Write on the whiteboard:
```
1. What are you building? (one sentence)
2. What should it look like / do when it's done?
3. What's the FIRST thing you need Codex to create?
```

**Say:** "Start with one prompt. Get that working. Then build on it. Don't try to describe the entire project in one giant prompt."

### Instructor Circulation Strategy

Divide your time:

- **Minutes 0-15:** Walk quickly to every student/pair. Confirm they have a plan and a first prompt. Unblock anyone who's stuck at the starting line.
- **Minutes 15-45:** Deeper help. Sit with students who are struggling. Help them refine prompts, debug issues, and iterate.
- **Minutes 45-75:** Focus on polish. Help students who are close to done add finishing touches. Encourage those behind to scope down to something presentable.
- **Minutes 75-105:** Five-minute warning at 90 minutes. Final touches only.

### Common Issues and Solutions

**"I don't know what to build."**
Ask: "What's your favorite class? What do you spend time on outside school? Let's build something connected to that." If still stuck, default to the portfolio page.

**"Codex isn't doing what I want."**
Review their prompt together. Apply the WHAT-WHERE-HOW-VERIFY formula from Day 2. Often the fix is adding specificity.

**"It made something but it looks bad."**
"Tell Codex what you don't like about it. Be specific. 'The colors are ugly' is okay, but 'Change the background to dark blue and the text to white' is better."

**"I'm done and it's only been 30 minutes."**
Stretch goals:
- Add more features or pages
- Make it responsive (works on mobile)
- Add animations or interactivity
- Use Codex to write a README explaining the project
- Help a neighbor who's stuck

**"Everything broke and I want to start over."**
"Don't start over. Ask Codex to fix it. Tell it exactly what's wrong. Starting over wastes the work you've already done."

**Context running out mid-project:**
"Use /compact to free up space, or start a fresh Codex session with a focused prompt that includes the current state of your project."

### Energy and Momentum Tips

- At the 30-minute mark, do a quick "pulse check" out loud: "Raise your hand if you have something working." Celebrate the hands.
- At the 60-minute mark: "You have 45 minutes left. If you're behind, simplify. Something finished beats something ambitious but broken."
- At the 80-minute mark: "Start wrapping up. Get it to a point you can show. We're presenting in 20 minutes."
- Play background music if appropriate for the classroom setting. Lo-fi or instrumental keeps energy steady without being distracting.

### Five-Minute Warning
**Say:** "Five minutes. Save your work. If Codex is in the middle of something, let it finish. Get ready to share."

## 11:00 - 11:30 | Presentations (30 min)

### Format
Each student or pair gets 2-3 minutes. With a class of 20, expect about 10-12 presentations (pairs reduce the count, and some may pass).

### Structure for Each Presentation
Guide students with these prompts on the projector:

```
1. What did you build? (10 seconds)
2. Show it working. (30-60 seconds)
3. What was your best prompt? (15 seconds)
4. What was the hardest part? (15 seconds)
```

**Say before presentations begin:** "This is a celebration, not a critique. Applaud every project. Everyone built something that didn't exist three hours ago."

### Voting
After all presentations, do two votes by show of hands:

- **Most Creative** -- the project that surprised you the most
- **Most Useful** -- the project you'd actually use in your life

Announce winners. If you have small prizes (stickers, candy, extra credit if applicable), hand them out. But make sure every student feels accomplished -- the real win is that everyone built something.

**If running behind:** Limit presentations to 90 seconds each. Cut the hardest-part question. Or have only 5-6 volunteers present instead of everyone.

## 11:30 - 11:50 | The Future Talk (20 min)

### Where AI Agents Are Heading (7 min)
**Say:** "Two and a half days ago, most of you had never used an AI agent. Now you've installed one, built projects with it, and you understand how it works under the hood. Let me tell you where this is going."

**Talking points:**
- AI agents will become as common as apps. You'll have agents that manage your email, schedule, files, and workflows.
- The tools will get easier. What takes a terminal command today will be a button tomorrow.
- Agents will connect to everything. MCP is just the beginning -- imagine your AI agent talking to your calendar, your school system, your music library, your car.
- Multi-agent systems are coming. Not one AI helper, but a team of specialized AI agents working together.

**Say:** "You're learning this at exactly the right time. The people who understand agents now will have a huge advantage in five years."

### Skills That Matter (7 min)
**Say:** "So what should you get good at? Here are the skills that matter MORE because of AI, not less."

Write on the whiteboard:
```
1. Prompt engineering -- talking to AI effectively
2. Critical thinking -- evaluating AI output, knowing when it's wrong
3. Creativity -- AI can execute, but YOU have to imagine
4. Communication -- explaining what you want clearly
5. Ethics and judgment -- knowing when to use AI and when not to
6. Domain expertise -- knowing YOUR field so you can direct the AI
```

**Say:** "Notice something? These are all HUMAN skills. AI handles the execution. You provide the direction, the judgment, and the creativity."

### The Quote (3 min)
**Say:** "I want to leave you with one idea."

Write on the whiteboard or display:

```
AI won't replace you.
But someone who knows how to use AI might.
```

**Let it sit for a moment.**

**Say:** "This isn't about fear. It's about opportunity. You now know how to use AI agents. That puts you ahead of most adults right now. Keep learning. Keep experimenting. Keep building."

### Resources to Keep Learning (3 min)
**Display a list or hand out printed cards:**

- **This repo** -- everything from this course lives here. Revisit anytime.
- **OpenAI documentation** -- docs.openai.com
- **Codex command reference** -- `codex --help` or the guide files in this repo
- **YouTube** -- search "OpenAI Codex tutorials" for video walkthroughs
- **Practice** -- the best way to learn is to use Codex on real tasks. Homework, personal projects, curiosity-driven exploration.
- **Community** -- OpenAI community forums, Reddit r/ChatGPT, Discord servers

**Say:** "The handouts you got yesterday have all the essential commands and prompt formulas. Keep them. Tape them to your wall. Use them."

## 11:50 - 12:00 | Certificates, Feedback, and Goodbye (10 min)

### Certificates (4 min)
If you prepared certificates of completion, hand them out now. Even a simple printed certificate with their name matters more than you'd think to this age group.

**Say as you hand them out:** "You earned this. You went from zero to building real projects with an AI agent in two and a half days."

### Quick Feedback (3 min)
**Say:** "Before you go -- I want to get better at teaching this. Can you write on a sticky note two things: one thing you liked and one thing you'd change. Don't put your name."

Collect the sticky notes. Read a few out loud if time permits and if they're positive -- it creates a good closing energy.

### Goodbye (3 min)
**Say:** "Thank you for being here. You learned what AI really is, how agents work, how to talk to them effectively, how to stay safe, and how to build real things. You're not just consumers of technology anymore -- you're people who can direct AI to create things that didn't exist before."

**Pause.**

**Say:** "Go build something cool. And when you do, come back and tell me about it."

## Instructor Notes for Day 3

### Timing Flexibility
- The biggest risk on Day 3 is the final project running over. Protect the presentation time -- it's the emotional payoff of the course.
- If the project lab is running long, give a hard stop at 10:50 and do abbreviated presentations.
- The Future Talk can be shortened to 10 minutes if needed. Hit the key quote and the skills list -- those are the ideas that stick.
- If a student genuinely needs 5 more minutes on their project, let them keep working during the first few presentations and present last.

### What Usually Goes Wrong
1. **A student's project doesn't work in time for presentations.** Have them present what they tried and what they learned. The attempt is valid.
2. **Presentations run long.** Gently enforce the time limit. "That's amazing -- time for our next presenter!"
3. **A student doesn't want to present.** Don't force it. Ask if they'd be willing to show their screen while you describe it. If not, respect it.
4. **Everyone votes for their friends.** This is fine. The voting is for fun, not for a grade.

### Post-Course
- Save the feedback sticky notes. Photograph them for your records.
- Note which projects students built -- this tells you what resonated.
- If any student showed exceptional engagement, consider connecting them with further resources or mentorship opportunities.
- Update the course materials based on what you learned. What ran too long? What should you cut? What questions came up that you weren't prepared for?

### Final Thought for the Instructor
The most important thing students take away isn't Codex commands or prompting formulas. It's the feeling that they can direct AI to do real things. That feeling of agency (pun intended) is what will drive them to keep learning long after the course ends. If they leave feeling empowered, you've done your job.
